01 About

I am a master's student at the Bernstein Center for Computational Neuroscience in Berlin. My training has been focused on how neural systems encode information, navigate uncertainty, and settle into stable states. I am drawn more generally to how adaptive systems search large, dynamic spaces. Comptuational neuroscience and machine learning have developed rich tools for characterizing attractor dynamics and suboptimal equilibria. I am interested in how the same principles apply to human collectives. My current thinking centers on collective adaptation--particularly the conditions under which human groups can fall into or escape suboptimal consensus. How do path dependence, social ties, and information environments shape the trajectories of groups and societies?

For my master's thesis, I am applying agent-based models to explore how human social norms alter the exploration-exploitation tradeoff, and the conditions which lead toward or away from collective learning traps. Through lab rotations, I have studied learning and emergent phenomena theoretically, experimentally, and in various model systems. In Henning Sprekeler's group, I am applying computer vision and behavioral modeling to understand how rodents learn sequential tasks. Working with Paula Kuokkanen, I built spiking network models to link population-level neural dynamics with far-field signals. Prior to my master's, I worked at Boston Children's Hospital, where I developed EEG analysis methods to index neural signatures underlying behavioral diagnoses of neural disorders.
